# Autoscaling signal comparison (FL-T006)

**Simulation, not production.** Every number in this report comes from a
seeded `fleetlab.dynamics` G/G/c simulation whose capacity/latency ground
truth is the FL-T004 fitted, **G8-within-error** profile
(`mock-loopback-cpu-dev__mock-8b__gateway-mock-flags-v1-conncap2`) — a
CPU-only **mock backend**, profiled for loop-mechanics purposes, not a real
GPU or CPU serving stack. See `docs/notes/fitting-method.md` for that
profile's own scope restrictions. This report answers "which signal detects
overload earliest, most stably, and with the fewest false alarms, in this
simulation" — it is evidence for a design recommendation, not a claim about
any production fleet.

**Hypothesis under test** (`docs/experiments.md` #1, source-reported as of
2026-07): GPU utilization is not a reliable overload signal for LLM
inference; predicted-goodput deficit and queue depth detect overload
earlier and with fewer false positives than GPU utilization. This report
tests that hypothesis on `chat-short`, `mixed`, and `bursty` (plus one
explicitly-labeled illustrative severe-overload variant, `bursty-
illustrative-severe`) and finds it **supported**, with two concrete,
reproducible mechanisms (§4, §5) rather than a restated assumption.

Full method, fairness protocol, and design rationale:
`docs/adr/0003-signal-comparison-design.md`. Raw output (seeded,
reproducible, embeds input digests):
`reports/scenarios/autoscaling-signals.json`, generated by
`python3 -m fleetlab.signals.build_signal_comparison`. Code:
`fleetlab/signals/`. Tests: `tests/signals/` (29 tests, known-answer +
determinism).

## 1. Ground truth and method, in brief

- **System:** 2 concurrency slots (`num_servers=2`, the fitted profile's
  disclosed client-side concurrency cap), mean per-request service time
  0.0765 s, derived as `num_servers / capacity_rps` from the fitted
  capacity (26.157 ± 1.233 rps) — held fixed across every scenario.
- **Six signals**, computed every 1 s from the completed simulation trace:
  `cpu_utilization` and `gpu_utilization` (**the same busy-concurrency-
  slot-fraction proxy** — see the caveat in §2), `queue_depth` (waiting
  only), `in_flight_requests` (waiting + in service), `token_arrival_rate`
  (10 s trailing window, weighted by each request's sampled input+output
  token count), `predicted_goodput_deficit` (10 s trailing offered-rate
  estimate minus the fitted `capacity_rps`, clamped at zero).
- **One shared tuning/detection procedure** for every signal in every
  scenario: threshold = baseline mean + 3·std over a scenario-specific
  quiet calibration window; a signal must sustain a crossing for 5
  continuous seconds to fire (debounce) and drop to ≤ 0.7× threshold for 5
  continuous seconds to clear (hysteresis). Nothing is hand-tuned per
  signal (ADR-0003).
- **Four scenarios**, same system throughout: `chat-short` (steady 8 rps,
  600 s — a "must stay quiet" baseline), `mixed` (steady 5 rps with 70/30
  chat/RAG-length mixture, 1200 s — a second, more token-heterogeneous
  baseline), `bursty` (real IB-T003 fixture, 60 s@2 rps + 15 s@20 rps
  repeating, 600 s — a **near-saturation, not hard-overload**, stress test:
  20 rps is 76.5% of the fitted capacity), and `bursty-illustrative-severe`
  (**`basis: assumed`**, NOT measured — the same real cycle with only the
  burst rate amplified 1.6× to 32 rps, which does exceed capacity,
  constructed because the real corpus has no scenario where offered load
  actually breaches this system's fitted capacity).

## 2. The CPU-vs-GPU-utilization caveat, stated up front

fleetlab's evidence corpus is CPU-only (a mock backend on a local dev
container); it carries no separate CPU or GPU occupancy telemetry at all.
`cpu_utilization` and `gpu_utilization` are therefore **the identical
simulated proxy** in every scenario in this report (fraction of the 2
concurrency slots occupied) — asserted structurally, not just claimed in
prose (`tests/signals/test_build_signal_comparison.py::
test_cpu_and_gpu_utilization_are_the_identical_proxy_series`). This is a
real limitation of this pass, recorded rather than papered over: nothing
here demonstrates a difference *between* CPU and GPU utilization as
signals, only a shared property of "concurrency-slot-occupancy" signals in
general (which real GPU utilization is one instance of).

## 3. Headline results

| Scenario | offered vs. capacity | true overload windows | `predicted_goodput_deficit` fires | `cpu`/`gpu`_utilization fires | `queue_depth` fires |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| `chat-short` (8 rps steady) | 31% of capacity | 0 | never | never (threshold **1.40 > 1.0**, unreachable) | never |
| `mixed` (5 rps steady) | 19% of capacity | 0 | never | never (threshold **1.03 > 1.0**, unreachable) | never |
| `bursty` (real, 20 rps burst) | 76.5% of capacity at peak | 0 | never | 6/8 burst cycles (informational only — no true overload to score against) | 1/8 burst cycles |
| `bursty-illustrative-severe` (32 rps burst, ASSUMED) | 122% of capacity at peak | 8 | **all 8, lag 12–13 s** | all 8, lag 5 s (debounce floor) | all 8, lag 5–9 s |

**Flapping under steady, non-overloaded load:** zero false triggers for
every one of the six signals across `chat-short` and `mixed`'s combined
1400 s of held-out evaluation window (the calibration window itself is
excluded from this count). `bursty`'s one queue-depth trigger during a
nominally "quiet" window (§5) is a labeling artifact, not baseline-noise
flapping.

## 4. Finding 1 — utilization's own tuned threshold is unreachable

Applying the *same* threshold rule used for every other signal
(mean + 3·std over a quiet baseline) to `cpu_utilization`/`gpu_utilization`
produces a threshold **greater than 1.0 — the signal's own physical
maximum — in three of the four scenarios** (`chat-short`: 1.40; `mixed`:
1.03; `bursty`: 1.01; only `bursty-illustrative-severe`, whose baseline is
itself busier, gets a reachable 0.35). The reason is not noisy code: with
only 2 concurrency slots, 1-second-sampled busy-fraction readings are
discretized to exactly `{0, 0.5, 1.0}` rather than continuous — at
`chat-short`'s 8 rps (31% true utilization), 200 baseline samples split
103/68/29 across those three values, giving a **standard deviation (0.36)
larger than the mean (0.32)**. A uniform 3-sigma rule, applied honestly,
renders the utilization signal **structurally incapable of ever firing**
under these conditions, however overloaded the system later becomes —
independent of and prior to any question about what the signal measures.
Low-concurrency systems (small `max_num_seqs` / few GPUs per replica) are
exactly where this bites hardest, and where continuous-batching engines
report high-looking utilization from a handful of concurrent streams.

## 5. Finding 2 — utilization pins near its ceiling well before true overload

In the real `bursty` scenario (76.5% utilization at peak, **zero true
overload** by the fitted-capacity ground truth), `cpu_utilization`'s
pooled burst-phase reading is already **p95 = 1.0, max = 1.0** (mean
70.8%) — indistinguishable, from a utilization reading alone, from what a
genuine saturation event would also show. Over the same phase,
`predicted_goodput_deficit` correctly stays at exactly 0 throughout (mean,
p95, and max all 0.0 — it is anchored to the true fitted capacity, so it
never mistakes 76.5%-of-capacity load for overload), while `queue_depth`
and `in_flight_requests` continue to read **graduated, unbounded** values
that scale with the load increase (queue depth mean 0.033→1.56, max
0→14; in-flight mean 0.20→2.98, max 9→16) without ever pinning at a
ceiling. This is the concrete mechanism behind the source-reported
hypothesis: utilization compresses its entire useful range into the
region just below saturation, precisely where an autoscaling decision
needs the most resolution, while count-based and deficit-based signals
keep discriminating.

## 6. Finding 3 — under genuine overload, instantaneous signals react at the debounce floor; the deficit signal is slower but exactly attributable

In `bursty-illustrative-severe` (32 rps burst, 122% of fitted capacity,
8 repeating cycles), every signal detects every one of the 8 true overload
windows (0 misses). But the *lag* differs meaningfully:

- `cpu_utilization`/`gpu_utilization`, `in_flight_requests`,
  `token_arrival_rate`: lag = **5.0 s in all 8 cycles** — exactly the
  shared debounce floor (the fastest any signal *can* react under the
  fairness protocol's shared debounce parameter); these are effectively
  "instantaneous" once the burst starts.
- `queue_depth`: lag = **5.0–9.0 s** (mean 5.9 s) — slightly noisier
  because its baseline threshold is itself near zero (degenerate,
  `queue_depth` is essentially always 0 at 7.7% baseline utilization), so
  small sampling jitter in exactly when the queue first forms shifts the
  debounce window's start by a sample or two.
- `predicted_goodput_deficit`: lag = **12–13 s (mean 12.6 s)** —
  systematically slower, and *exactly* explainable: its 10 s trailing
  window must accumulate enough burst-rate arrivals for the windowed
  average to cross the fitted capacity (26.16 rps), which — solving the
  window-mix arithmetic for this scenario's baseline/burst rates — takes
  ≈8 s after burst onset, plus the shared 5 s debounce ≈ 13 s. This is the
  direct, quantified cost of anchoring the signal to a *smoothed estimate
  of true capacity* rather than an instantaneous state variable: it never
  false-fires (Finding 2), but it pays for that specificity with the one
  scenario in this report where it is not the fastest signal.

## 7. Signal-by-signal summary (responsiveness / stability / failure mode)

| Signal | Responsiveness (this report) | Stability (flapping) | When it fails |
|---|---|---|---|
| `cpu_utilization` / `gpu_utilization` | Fast when its threshold is reachable (5 s, debounce floor) | No false triggers observed here, but only because its tuned threshold is usually unreachable (Finding 1) | Threshold unreachable at low/moderate concurrency under uniform tuning (Finding 1); pins near its 1.0 ceiling well before true saturation, losing discrimination exactly when it matters (Finding 2); **cannot be distinguished from CPU utilization in this corpus at all** (§2) |
| `queue_depth` | Fast (5–9 s) | 1 trigger in a labeled-quiet window, traced to post-burst drain-down bleeding into the next baseline phase (a labeling artifact, not noise) | Its own baseline is near-zero by construction at low utilization, so its tuned threshold is also near-zero — very sensitive, but blind to any admission control that sheds excess load instead of queueing it (this simulator never sheds in these scenarios; a real gateway with a bounded queue would make `queue_depth` under-read the true offered overload) |
| `in_flight_requests` | Fast (5 s) | None observed | Conflates "many short requests briefly queued" with "few long-running requests genuinely occupying capacity" — this report's fixed mean service time does not exercise that distinction, but it is a known real-world failure mode (long-output workloads inflate in-flight count without any queueing) |
| `token_arrival_rate` | Fast (5 s) here | None observed | In this pass it closely tracks request-count-based signals (§8) because none of the three real workloads vary their length distribution *over time* — only arrival *rate* varies (bursty) or the mixture is static (mixed); its distinguishing value (catching load shifts invisible to request-count alone) is not exercised by this workload set |
| `predicted_goodput_deficit` | Slowest under genuine overload (12–13 s, Finding 3) but the only signal that is exactly zero and never false-fires under sub-capacity load (Finding 2) | Perfect: 0 flapping, 0 false positives in every scenario | Wholly inherits the fitted profile's own uncertainty (this profile's stated capacity error is ~5%, and the corpus is a single CPU-only mock bucket — see `docs/notes/fitting-method.md`); requires a maintained fitted-profile pipeline that the other five signals do not need; if the underlying profile drifts (engine/hardware change) this signal drifts silently with it |

## 8. Token-arrival-rate: a documented scope limit, not a claimed result

`token_arrival_rate` is included per the task's signal list, but this
report's three workloads (`chat-short`, `mixed`, `bursty`) each hold their
length distribution **constant over time** — only the arrival *rate*
varies (bursty) or the mixture composition is fixed (mixed, 70/30
throughout). Under fixed per-request service time (this task's system
model, ADR-0003), `token_arrival_rate` is therefore a (noisier) rescaling
of the request-count arrival rate, and this report found no scenario where
it diverged meaningfully from `in_flight_requests`/`queue_depth`'s
behavior. Its real comparative advantage — catching a load-composition
shift (e.g. a swing toward `gen-long-out`-style long completions) that
request-count signals would miss entirely — is not exercised here. This is
recorded as a scope limitation, not a positive finding this report did not
earn.

## 9. Recommendation

1. **Primary signal: `predicted_goodput_deficit`.** It is the only signal
   in this report that is provably specific (zero false positives across
   every scenario, including two dedicated flapping-under-steady-load
   tests) because it is anchored to the fitted, G8-validated capacity
   rather than to another signal's own noisy baseline. Its cost is a
   real, quantified detection lag under sudden severe overload (~13 s here)
   and a dependency on a maintained fitted-profile pipeline — pair it with:
2. **Secondary/fallback signal: `queue_depth`** (or `in_flight_requests`).
   Both are fast (5–9 s here), require no fitted-profile maintenance, and
   use Contract-2-stable metric names — a reasonable fallback if the
   fitted profile becomes stale or unavailable, with the explicit caveat
   that they under-read true overload if the platform sheds excess load
   instead of queueing it (not exercised in these scenarios).
3. **Do not use `cpu_utilization`/`gpu_utilization` alone as a scale-out
   trigger** at low-to-moderate concurrency, consistent with the
   source-reported hypothesis this report set out to test: Finding 1 shows
   a uniformly-tuned threshold is frequently unreachable, and Finding 2
   shows it pins near its ceiling well before genuine saturation even when
   reachable. It remains useful as a **secondary/diagnostic** signal
   (e.g. corroborating a `queue_depth` trigger), never as the sole trigger.
4. **`token_arrival_rate`** is not yet differentiated from request-count
   signals by this report (§8) — revisit once a length-varying-over-time
   workload (e.g. a `gen-long-out` composition shift) is exercised.

## 10. Limitations

- Simulation ≠ production (program-wide pledge). The ground-truth system
  is a CPU-only mock backend at 2 concurrency slots; a real GPU serving
  stack at higher concurrency has continuous, not discretized, utilization
  readings, which would change (but not necessarily reverse) Finding 1's
  magnitude.
- `bursty-illustrative-severe` is `basis: assumed`, not measured — it
  demonstrates a mechanism the real corpus does not reach, not a measured
  result.
- CPU and GPU utilization are mechanistically identical in this report
  (§2) — no evidence here distinguishes them.
- The debounce/threshold parameters (`k=3`, 5 s debounce, 0.7 clear
  fraction, 10 s window) are one reasonable, uniformly-applied choice, not
  a sweep over the parameter space; a different shared choice would shift
  absolute lag numbers (though the *relative* findings — utilization's
  unreachable threshold, the deficit signal's specificity/lag trade-off —
  are robust to the specific parameter values, since they follow from the
  signals' underlying shapes, not from this particular `k`/debounce pair).
- Fitted-capacity ground truth carries FL-T004's own stated uncertainty
  (±1.23 rps / ~4.7%) and is a single mock-backend bucket; every number in
  this report inherits that.
